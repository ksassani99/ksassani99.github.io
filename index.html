01

High-Voltage
Isolated Sensing Interface

Formula Electric · FSAE
JAN 2026 — PRESENT

A single PCB that measures voltage and current across a 588V tractive system without ever letting the high-voltage rail touch the low-voltage microcontroller. Three isolation boundaries (resistive, power, and digital) in one board.

The architecture splits the PCB into two galvanically separated domains. On the HV side, a precision six-element 120:1 resistive divider scales 420–588V down to a 3.47–4.85V signal, buffered through an OPA192 unity-gain op-amp into a 16-bit ADC. Current is sensed with two Hall-effect ICs: one coarse on the 5V domain for peak traction loads, and one sensitive on the 3V3 domain for cruising, both buffered and digitized.

A UCC12040 isolated DC/DC carries a clean 5V across the boundary to power the HV-side analog front end, while an ISO1640 bidirectional I2C isolator and a TXS0102 level shifter let the LV microcontroller talk to the ADC over a single I2C bus. A LM1117 LDO provides the 3V3 rail. The result is a compact, clean sensor interface that turns a scary voltage into trustworthy numbers.

ArchitectureTwo galvanically separated domains; all HV-to-LV signals cross through resistive, power, and digital isolation boundaries into one I²C output bus
SensingSix-element 120:1 divider scales 420–588 V into a 3.47–4.85 V signal, buffered by an OPA192 op-amp into a 16-bit Δ-Σ ADC; dual-range Hall-effect current sensing captures both peak and cruising loads
PowerUCC12040 isolated DC/DC carries a clean 5 V across the barrier to the HV-side analog front end, with a local LDO dropping to 3V3 for the digital domain
Comms / I/OBidirectional I²C isolator plus a 5V↔3V3 level shifter bridges the HV-side ADC to the LV microcontroller; a 6-pin Duraclik feeds the vehicle harness
ScopeOwned schematic and layout end to end; board revision 3/10/2026 out for fabrication, bring-up queued on return

02

Autonomous
Integration Module

Formula Electric · FSAE
SEPT 2024 — MAY 2025

A sensor-fusion and vehicle-state board, the central nervous system between the car's motion sensors and its control electronics. IMU, wheel speed, brake command, and status lighting, all on one STM32-plugin carrier over CAN.

AIM integrates a BNO055 9-DOF IMU with dual quadrature wheel-speed interfaces, where each sensor outputs phase-shifted square waves: frequency encodes speed, phase encodes direction. A dedicated level-shifting and filtering network conditions the signals for the MCU. Status illumination is driven through six NMOS low-side switches with 10k pulldowns, providing deterministic state when the MCU isn't actively driving the bus.

Power splits across a TPSM84205 (24V → 5V) and TPSM84212 (24V → 12V) for the heavier rails, with an LM1117 LDO dropping to 3V3 for the digital domain. The board also runs dual CAN bus channels, an SD card logger, and a PWM-driven brake-light output. Bringing up this many interfaces on one board was my first real lesson in BOM discipline and layout planning.

ArchitectureSTM32 plugin carrier fusing IMU, wheel-speed, status lighting, and brake command into a single CAN node on the vehicle bus
Sensing9-DOF IMU on the I²C sensor bus paired with dual quadrature wheel-speed interfaces; phase-shifted square waves are conditioned through a level-shifting and filtering network and decoded in firmware
PowerHybrid rail tree: TPSM84205 (24V→5V) and TPSM84212 (24V→12V) buck modules handle heavy rails, with an LM1117 LDO generating the 3V3 digital supply
Comms / I/ODual CAN1 / CAN2 channels, SPI SD-card logger, six NMOS low-side status lights with 10k pulldowns, and a PWM-driven brake-light output
ScopeOwned schematic and layout; the integrated vehicle-state stack cut overall positioning error by roughly 75% versus the prior split-board sensor chain

03

Space Invaders
Handheld Console

Coursework (HOPE) · Team Project
DEC 2025 w/ Rahil Pasha, Lucas Bates, Zena Wu

A Game Boy–style handheld on a custom 2-layer PCB, built around an ESP32-S3 and a 1.8″ LCD. It actually plays Space Invaders. A course project about form-factor discipline, signal integrity at low cost, and the joy of a board that fits in your palm.

Compute runs on an ESP32-S3-WROOM-1 programmed through the Arduino Core, driving a 1.8-inch ST7735 TFT over SPI (TFT-eSPI library) and a K50 8Ω speaker through a PAM8302A Class-D mono amp on I²S. The display is 320×240×16-bit color at a frame rate generous enough for arcade sprites and smooth alien descents.

Inputs are a three-button cluster (Start, Reset, Boot) with RC low-pass debouncing and a Bourns PEC11R rotary encoder with integrated push-switch, decoded in firmware via quadrature. Power comes from three AAA cells in series feeding an LD1117S33 3V3 LDO, with a USB-C receptacle for programming and a pair of PMEG3030 Schottky diodes isolating the USB and battery rails so the two supplies can coexist safely.

AudioPAM8302A Class-D mono amp (3.2 W, 9WLP) driven over I²S, feeding a K50 8 Ω speaker
